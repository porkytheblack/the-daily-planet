EU passes landmark AI transparency act — companies have ninety days

The European Parliament voted 412-89 to enact the most comprehensive AI regulation in history. The implications for open-source development, enterprise compliance, and the global race to govern artificial intelligence are profound.

MK
Mara Kessler Senior Correspondent
Filed 6:58 AM EAT
8 min read

The AI Transparency and Accountability Act passed the European Parliament with a decisive supermajority on Friday evening Brussels time. The vote — 412 in favor, 89 against, with 32 abstentions — marks the culmination of two years of legislative debate and positions Europe as the first major economy to mandate architectural disclosure for AI systems.

Unlike previous attempts at AI regulation, which focused primarily on downstream outcomes and behavioral compliance, this act reaches into the technical substrate itself. Companies deploying AI systems within the EU must now disclose training data provenance, model architecture details, and a comprehensive accounting of deployment contexts — including downstream applications built on their APIs.

"This isn't a content regulation. It's a structural transparency mandate. The difference matters." — Commissioner for Digital Affairs, European Commission

What changes for developers

The regulation introduces three compliance tiers based on model capability and deployment scale. Foundation models with over 10 billion parameters fall into the highest tier, requiring quarterly disclosure reports and external audit access. Smaller models face lighter requirements, though the threshold parameters remain contentious among industry groups.

Open-source projects face a nuanced exemption framework. Models distributed under approved open licenses may qualify for reduced disclosure requirements, provided they maintain publicly accessible model cards that meet the act's minimum specification. The details of this framework will be determined by technical standards bodies over the coming months.

The 90-day timeline

Perhaps the most aggressive element is the compliance timeline. Companies have 90 days from enactment to submit initial disclosure documents. Full compliance is expected within 12 months. Penalties for non-compliance scale with global revenue — up to 4% for the highest tier, mirroring the GDPR enforcement structure that proved effective in data privacy.

Editor's Analysis
This is the first regulation to mandate architectural disclosure, not just behavioral compliance. Watch how this affects open-source model development — the exemption framework could either accelerate or chill community research.
Related Context
The U.S. has proposed similar legislation three times since 2024. Each attempt stalled in committee. The EU's passage may accelerate transatlantic regulatory convergence.
